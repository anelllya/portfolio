# TankNET

**Brand identity · Website · LinkedIn · Acrolon Technologies**

![TankNET tradeshow booth](../../img/tanknet/tanknet-cover.webp){.postCover }

---

## The context

Acrolon Technologies has been building fermentation management hardware and software for over 25 years. Their controllers are installed in more than 400 wineries and breweries, from small craft operations to Opus One, Robert Mondavi, and Jackson Family Wines.

The reputation was built on product quality. The brand had kept pace with the engineering, not the market. The logo was consistent but dated: the graphic on the E read too close to Internet Explorer, and the colors did not hold up against the black controller fronts in cellar conditions. The website had the right information but not the right architecture. Visitors who wanted to go deeper had nowhere to go.

I joined in January 2025 as Lead Design Consultant with full ownership of the brand refresh, website, and LinkedIn.

---

## The logo

The brief was practical: the mark needed to work at the size of a controller front panel label and at the size of a tradeshow backdrop. Engraved on hardware. Printed on vinyl. Rendered on screen.

![Old TankNET logo](../../img/tanknet/logo-old.webp)

We found a geometric, industrial typeface that reads as technical without being cold. I made optical corrections to the spacing between the N-K-N sequence, a subtle adjustment that keeps the mark balanced at any size. The circular favicon mark was derived from the same font geometry, with enough modification that it reads as a distinct symbol rather than just initials.

![New TankNET logo](../../img/tanknet/logo-new.webp)

Since the rebrand, every controller shipped carries the new mark. The brand now lives in cellars and brewhouses alongside the hardware.

## The website

The old site had the right information in the wrong structure. Winery and brewery customers were addressed in the same paragraphs, on the same pages. Visitors who wanted more detail had nowhere to go.

![Old website](../../img/tanknet/website-old.webp)

The new site is built around progressive disclosure. It starts with the two clearest customer entry points, wineries and breweries, each with their own dedicated landing page, their own language, their own path to a quote.

From there the site moves through trust signals in deliberate order:

**Famous customers in the industry:** names that serious buyers recognise immediately.

**Numbers that build confidence:** 400+ clients, 35,000+ controlled tanks, 25% average energy savings, 25 years in business.

**One system, two traditions:** a clean split that directs each visitor to the information relevant to them.

**The full product lineup:** hardware and software presented together as a coherent system.

**Feature depth:** pump-overs, pulse cooling, Thread mesh protocol, the AI initiative and where it is going.

**Customer stories:** the people behind the numbers.

We kept everything that was working and built around it. The goal was a complete picture between hardware and software — the kind of site a prospective customer can spend real time in.

![New website](../../img/tanknet/website-new.webp)

When the site went live, someone who had known the company for years said it looked like the company had been acquired.

It had not been acquired. It had just started looking like itself.

## The tradeshow assets

TankNET exhibits at Unified Wine and Grape Symposium and CBC — two of the largest industry events in the US wine and beer markets.

The booth needed to carry the new brand into a physical space with confidence. Navy backdrop, clean typography, product photography and feature copy all consistent with the new brand language.

## LinkedIn

I manage the company LinkedIn presence and create all content. The current series — Behind The Fermentation — goes six episodes deep on the technology, craft, and people behind fermentation management.

Building a content voice for a 25-year-old technical company is a different problem than building one for a startup. The audience already knows the product. The content has to earn attention by being genuinely useful, not just promotional.
